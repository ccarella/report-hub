The Dukes of Hazzard: A Campy Car-Chase Icon with a Complicated Legacy

February 8, 2026
  • "The Dukes of Hazzard" ran for seven seasons (1979–1985) on CBS and was once one of the most-watched shows on American television.
  • The series followed cousins Bo and Luke Duke, on probation for moonshine running, outsmarting corrupt county commissioner Boss Hogg in fictional Hazzard County, Georgia.
  • The orange 1969 Dodge Charger "General Lee" became an automotive pop‑culture icon, as did Daisy Duke's cutoff shorts and the show's country soundtrack.
  • The show sits in a strange cultural place today: remembered fondly as lighthearted rural escapism but criticized for its romanticized Confederate imagery.
  • Spinoffs, reunion movies, and a 2005 film adaptation tried to update the formula with mixed results, proving how hard it is to modernize Hazzard's tone.
  • Rewatching it now means separating the goofy stunts and family loyalty from the parts of its visual language that no longer land as harmless fun.

"The Dukes of Hazzard" is one of those shows that you don't just remember watching—you remember the sound of it. The Waylon Jennings theme song, the horn of the General Lee, the gravelly voiceovers calling Bo and Luke "those Duke boys" as the camera freezes mid‑jump. Airing on CBS from 1979 to 1985, the series ran for seven seasons and 147 episodes and at its peak ranked just behind "Dallas" in the Friday‑night ratings hierarchy. It was mass‑market comfort food: action‑comedy, chase scenes, and a cartoonish battle of wits between lovable rural outlaws and small‑town corruption.

The basic setup is simple. Cousins Bo and Luke Duke live on a family farm in fictional Hazzard County, Georgia, alongside their cousin Daisy and their moral anchor, Uncle Jesse. The boys are on probation for running moonshine; as part of the deal that kept them out of prison, they're banned from carrying firearms and from leaving the county without permission. That constraint becomes a narrative feature: instead of guns, they rely on compound bows (sometimes with dynamite‑tipped arrows) and high‑speed improvisation. Each episode drops them into some local scheme—often cooked up or exploited by county commissioner Jefferson Davis "Boss" Hogg and his bumbling sheriff Rosco P. Coltrane—and then watches them wriggle out with a mix of moral stubbornness and slapstick ingenuity.

The star of the show, though, is arguably not human. The General Lee, a bright‑orange 1969 Dodge Charger with a welded door frame, roll cage, and oversized "01" on the doors, became one of television's most recognizable vehicles. The show leaned fully into this: countless scenes open or close with the car flying off a dirt ramp, camera lingering mid‑air before cutting to a safe landing that real‑world physics would never allow. For kids and gearheads, the General Lee was a fantasy object, a symbol of speed and freedom; for television production, it was a famously consumable resource, with hundreds of Chargers reportedly wrecked over the course of filming.

Around that automotive core, the show built a kind of rural superhero ensemble. Daisy Duke, whose name would become shorthand for cutoff denim shorts, functions as both eye‑catching marketing hook and competent co‑conspirator, using charm and agility to get the family out of trouble. Uncle Jesse, in contrast, brings a stern but loving moral presence, reminding the boys that the fight against corruption can't justify becoming corrupt themselves. Boss Hogg and Rosco, meanwhile, are written almost as Looney Tunes villains: greedy, scheming, and perpetually humiliated, their most dangerous trait is not competence but institutional power.

Part of why "The Dukes of Hazzard" hit so big in its era is that it promised a particular vision of the American South: tight‑knit, poor but resourceful, suspicious of big‑city outsiders, and governed by a kind of informal folk justice. The Dukes might technically be criminals, but the show frames their original offense—moonshine running—as an almost victimless, even romantic, tradition. In episode after episode, their law‑breaking is justified by the idea that the official law is crooked. The camera never doubts that the Dukes are on the right side; the tension is in how they can stay one jump ahead of a system rigged against them.

That tone is also where the series runs into trouble for modern viewers. The General Lee's roof is emblazoned with the Confederate battle flag, and the car's name itself nods to Confederate General Robert E. Lee. When the show first aired, that imagery was often treated in mainstream pop culture as an apolitical Southern backdrop, stripped of its direct association with slavery and white supremacy. Today, that separation is much harder to defend. Reruns and merchandising have been pulled or softened in various contexts, and debates flare over whether the show can be enjoyed "for what it is" without endorsing the symbols it casually normalizes.

The franchise has tried multiple times to reboot or extend itself. Animated spinoffs, a short‑lived series focused on deputy Enos, reunion TV movies in the late 1990s and early 2000s, and a 2005 theatrical film all attempted to repackage Hazzard County for new audiences. Most of these projects leaned on heightened parody—turning the original's already broad humor into full‑on farce—or on nostalgia, counting on viewers' fondness for the theme song and the car jumps. The critical response was mixed at best, and none of the revivals managed to become essential in their own right. The formula works best inside the specific late‑'70s, early‑'80s television ecosystem that birthed it.

So what do you do with "The Dukes of Hazzard" in 2026? One option is to treat it as a time capsule: a reminder of how American TV once blended slapstick, car culture, and a flattening of Southern symbols into feel‑good primetime. Another is to actively interrogate it—to notice which parts still genuinely land (the stunts, the chemistry among the cast, the emphasis on family loyalty) and which elements now register as naive or harmful. The show can still be fun, but it is no longer harmless background noise; it asks viewers to decide what to carry forward from Hazzard County, and what to leave where it belongs: in syndication reruns and hazy memories of the "good ol' boys" who never meant no harm.
